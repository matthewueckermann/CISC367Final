Impact of Industrial Activity on Community Health¶

Matthew Ueckermann

Problem¶

I am interested in exploring how industrial pollution impacts the health of surrounding communities. Specifically, by following a thread of my midterm assignment where I investigated toxic chemical releases, I am curious how these releases correlate with markers of community health. Two main correlations which come to mind are those between carcinogen releases and cancer rates as well as the release of specific chemicals and asthma rates. I want to see if clear correlations exist between emissions and these health issues and if they do exist the magnitude of the effect.

Background¶

Various industries release or cause the release of chemicals and particulates which have the potential to directly and severely impact the health and lives of those in the surrounding community. Releases of toxic chemicals are often mitigated to some extent to meet local air pollution licensing requirements and to prevent negative press attention. However, due to the nature of the equipment used as well as the effectiveness of mitigation technology, some releases will occur. While other industrial activities, like mountain removal mining, cause the release of large amounts of particulates as well as toxic chemicals which cannot be easily controlled.

The fact that industries pollute chemicals which have been shown to be toxic in lab settings or which are believed to cause lung disorders, begs the question of whether these emissions have demonstrable effects on markers of community health. Do elevated quantities of exposure to these chemicals cause higher cancer rates? Or are chemicals emitted in small enough quantities that other factors dominate whether or not people develop certain disorders. This information equips communities with the knowledge about whether they should be concerned about surrounding industrial activity as well the extent to push for greater emission control or use of safer practices.

Research Questions¶

  • To what extent can the cities with higher than average rates of cancer be predicted by looking at carcinogen release from facilities in the same county?
  • Do any routes of chemical release (air, water, landfill) cause a greater associated rate of cancer?
  • To what extent can cities with higher than average rates of asthma be predicted by looking at rates of nitrogen oxide and particulate matter emissions?
  • Do communities with a more active mining sector have a greater associated rate of asthma then those that do not?

Justification¶

For the first question, carcinogens are by definition compounds which are believed to cause cancer. Exposure limits have historically been defined in terms of occupational exposure limits, as these are where people are generally exposed to large enough quantities of these compounds frequently enough for them to make a difference. However, it is less straightforward to define exposure quantities which could cause surrounding communities to develop cancer at higher rates. Some studies show that atmospheric carcinogens in the UK have been linked with higher child cancer rates [1]. Completing a similar study in the US could give residents important knowledge about the facilities that surround them.

The second question then builds on this to see if there is a noticeable difference based on emission route. As my midterm showed that the majority of emissions are emitted to the air, I was interested if the amount emitted to the water, impacts health in a more detrimental way.

For the third question, it is widely accepted that exposure to particulates as well as oxides can trigger asthma attacks and may also lead to the development of new-onset asthma in some people [2]. It would be interesting to characterize whether emissions from industrial facilities play a role in exacerbating this condition, or if the particulates and other compounds which trigger asthma can be primarily attributed to transportation - another major source of these compounds. This could equip residents with extra knowledge of what to attribute their asthma too and what types of facilities to avoid to prevent triggering it.

The fourth question builds off of the third, by investigating the impact of mining specifically on asthma. This is because some forms of mining, namely mountain strip top, have been associated with increased amount of respiratory disorders, including asthma [3]. This builds on the history of lung disorders associated with mining, notably black lung in coal miners. This could show that entire communities, not just workers, are impacted by exposure brought about by the mining industry.

[1] Knox, E.G. (2005). Childhood cancers and atmospheric carcinogens. Journal Epidemiol Community Healthy, 59, 101-105. http://dx.doi.org/10.1136/jech.2004.021675

[2] Guarnieri, M. and Balmes, J.R. (2014). Outdoor air pollution and asthma. Lancet, 383(9928), 1561-1592. https://doi.org/10.1016/S0140-6736(14)60617-6

[3] Hendryx, M. (2013). Personal and family health in rural areas of Kentucky with and without mountaintop coal mining. The Journal of Rural Health, 29, s79-288. https://doi-org.udel.idm.oclc.org/10.1111/jrh.12016

Ethical Concerns and Considerations¶

Some ethical concerns:

  • Failing to find a correlation between chemical releases and health concerns does not mean that they do not exist. Some stakeholders like chemical or mining companies could use this as evidence that their facilities do not impact the surrounding community when it really does. Residents with their health adversely effected, another stakeholder, may then have a harder time advocating for greater control of releases or winning much needed settlements from these companies.
    • Notably also, this analysis assumes that all residents in a city are exposed to emissions in their city at the same level, which is most definitely not the case. As one moves further away from the emissions point source, the concentration will rapidly decline. As such, people who live closer to a site (who are most likely come from less privileged communities) are at much more risk than people on the other side of the city. This adds a major limitation to the conclusions and as such should not be used to disvalue the experiences of people close to facilities.
  • Similar to above, finding a correlation between these chemical releases and health concerns also does not mean that they are real, as I am not controlling for covariates. Because of this the major stakeholders mentioned above cannot jump to any conclusions, specifically the residents stating that the surrounding industry is responsible for specific pollution. Instead, they should use this analysis as an impetus to complete more research in their specific community.
  • Other stakeholders involved are local hospitals and healthcare providers who may want knowledge how the industry around them impacts their patients. They may take into consideration the types of chemicals emitted by the industry in the surrounding area in the way they diagnose or certain disorders. As they might expect that a disorder might be one that has been connected to a chemical that is emitted in the community and might treat as such, even if that is not the case. Because of this, we will have to clarify that these are community results that should not be taken for diagnosis purposes.

Some data analysis considerations:

  • Covariates generally need to be controlled for in this type of analysis; however, this is not possible with the datasets available.
  • County wide data might dilute the impact of local facilities on community health.
    • Similarly, the PLACES dataset does warn against excessive extrapolation of the county results due to low sample sizes; therefore, results must be taken with some skepticism and should provide an impetus for further study.

Of course, this effect size is extremely small, and the fact that it is statistically significant most likely is only the case due to the large amount of data used. However, this does show that there is no clear correlation between emissions and cancer rate.

Another thing that may be interesting, is whether the means of cities with emissions are different than those without. As the age-adjusted cancer rate appears to be more or less normally distributed, we can use an ANOVA:

In [10]:
no_emissions = combined_cancer_loc[combined_cancer_loc['Total carcinogen release (lb)']==0.00002]['Age-adjusted cancer rate (%)']
emissions = combined_cancer_loc[combined_cancer_loc['Total carcinogen release (lb)']!=0.00002]['Age-adjusted cancer rate (%)'] 
f, p = f_oneway(no_emissions,emissions)
print("The p value is",p,"therefore the two population means are not statistically different")

The p value is 0.5419706121413186 therefore the two population means are not statistically different

This indicates that there is no statistically significant difference between cities that have carcinogen emissions and those that do not, which follows from the trends above.

Note that this is for all carcinogen emissions, grouping together all carcinogens, as well as routes of emissions. It is likely that some carcinogens are more potent than others, while some routes cause more exposure to people. My previous analysis showed that the majority of emissions go to the air. Thinking about how the concentration of a pollutant changes as one moves away from a facility, we know see that the concentration in the air would have a cubed dependence on distance, as the emissions should move in all directions forming a semi-sphere. As such, moving away from the plant quickly dilutes the concentration of any emitted chemical - making them less harmful. Therefore, air emissions most likely only impact people surrounding the facilities and not people on the other side of a town/city. This makes the previous results even less surprising.

Yet this opens a question for other forms of emission, mainly water emissions, where the main dilution that occurs immediately is as the chemical it enters the body of water, and therefore is not as dependent on how close a person lives to the facility, but the proximity to the body of water

Comparing to Emissions Data¶

Unfortunately, the TRI does not contain emissions data for major contributors to asthma, namely NOx and particulate matter (PM). Other government programs do monitor these pollutants, which typically result from combustion. As these irritants result from combustion, it is likely that people are more commonly exposed to them by vehicle and building exhaust than point sources like plants.

Regardless, it would still be interesting to know whether or not there is any correlation between point sources and asthma rates. Although the TRI does not record some of the most prevalent asthma inducing compounds, it is likely that total chemical emissions would scale with NOx and PM emissions for point sources. As NOx and most likely come from combustion - which likely scales with other emissions as more combustion (used to provide heat to a plant) likely indicates higher production rates and therefore higher levels of emissions. Appendix A gives a validation of this correlation, by using a different government dataset - which includes county data but not city data such that it could not be compared to the PLACEs dataset. This found a significant correlation between TRI total emissions for a county and NOx/PM emissions, with a pearson's correlation coefficient of ~0.35 for both.

Conclusion¶

This analysis failed to find strong correlations between the release of carcinogens and cancer rates or the release of chemical emissions both from all industries and specifically from coal mining and asthma rates when looking at city level data. The lack of strong correlations most likely indicates that city level data is not granular enough to see health outcomes associated with point source emissions. This is likely as all emissions are rapidly diluted as one moves away from said point source. Indicating that people close to the facility are subjected to significantly higher concentrations compared to those on the other side of the city. As such, the impact of elevated cancer or asthma rates for those close to the point source appears to be diluted by those who are not impacted. This could lead to the most likely incorrect conclusion that residents are not impacted by toxic chemical emissions at all. Likely instead, those who live closest to these facilities, who are also likely make up less privileged groups, are still impacted by emissions; however, moving away from the facility people are not impacted. Similarly, this work took all carcinogens to be the same in case of comparing to cancer rates, this is most likely not the case as some are more potent than others and could then have more negative impacts on release. While the data on coal mining was quite small and did not measure the correct indicators (like particulate matter) in order to truly represent the impact of activities like strip top mountain mining.

Regardless, this analysis could provide comfort to people who live in cities with a large amount of industrial activity - although not necessarily close to that activity - that it most likely does not impact their health.
